### Defintition
In 1969 in her writing in *On Death and Dying*, Dr. Elisabeth Kubler-Ross outlined a theory of the progression of grief: 

1. Denial – Refusal to accept the diagnosis or minimize its seriousness; difficulty processing the reality of the situation.
2. Anger – Frustration or resentment, often directed toward others, medical staff, loved ones, or fate.
3. Bargaining – Attempts to negotiate for more time or a different outcome (often spiritual or internal negotiations).
4. Depression – Sadness, withdrawal, hopelessness, or anticipatory grief.
5. Acceptance – A state of relative peace with the reality of the condition.

Kübler-Ross emphasized that these stages are not linear. Individuals may:
- Experience multiple stages simultaneously
- Move back and forth between stages
- Skip stages entirely
Although she later expanded her thinking, the five-stage model became the dominant popular framework for understanding grief.
### Critical Context
- Kübler-Ross originally developed the model to describe terminally ill patients confronting their own mortality, not individuals grieving the loss of someone else.
- When applied to bereavement, some stages—particularly bargaining—may function differently or appear less prominently.
- The model does not distinguish between healthy and pathological grief. It was never intended to be a strict psychological diagnostic tool but rather a descriptive framework based on clinical observations.
- Modern grief research tends to favor more fluid, individualized models rather than fixed stage progressions.

Because of its original intent, it may be more accurate to think of it as a model of the **psychological process of dying**, rather than a universal theory of grief.
