## Emergency Medical Services (EMS)

### Overview  
Emergency Medical Services (EMS) is a system of prehospital medical providers that deliver emergency care to the sick and injured. EMS clinicians also offer preventative education, early crisis identification, and community-based healthcare services.

---

### History  
Modern EMS in the United States began with the 1966 publication of *Accidental Death and Disability*, which exposed critical gaps in prehospital care and highlighted how timely interventions—such as those provided by Basic Life Support (BLS) and Advanced Life Support (ALS)—could save lives. This led to the **Highway Safety Act of 1966**, establishing federal leadership through the creation of the **National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA)**, which set standards for ambulance equipment, communication systems, and personnel qualifications.

In the 1970s, the Department of Transportation developed the first standardized EMT training curriculum. Since the 1990s, there has been a consistent federal effort to standardize EMS education across states. In 2019, NHTSA’s *EMS Agenda 2050* reaffirmed these goals and emphasized making EMS an attractive career choice for future professionals.

### National EMS Scope of Practice Model  
The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) establishes minimum skill requirements for each level of EMS provider. However, licensure and operational scope are determined at the **state level**. Local medical directors oversee daily operations and may issue standing orders.

#### Key Provider Roles  

- **Basic Life Support (BLS)**  
  Trained laypersons (e.g., teachers, security staff) who can perform CPR, use Automated External Defibrillators (AEDs), and apply basic bleeding control (e.g., tourniquets). Training typically lasts 4–10 hours.

- **Emergency Medical Responder (EMR)**  
  First responders trained in immediate care and patient stabilization. EMRs support EMTs and integrate into emergency response teams. Training is approximately 50 hours.

- **Emergency Medical Technician (EMT)**  
  Responsible for assessing, treating, stabilizing, and transporting patients to hospitals. EMTs ensure continuity of care and follow established chains of command. Training ranges from 200–250 hours.

- **Paramedic**  
  Trained in advanced life support (ALS) with extensive theoretical and clinical knowledge. Paramedics provide complex interventions such as airway management, medication administration, and advanced cardiac care. Training typically spans 2,000–2,500 hours.

#### Dispatch  
Public access to EMS begins at the **9-1-1 dispatch center**, where trained dispatchers connect callers with emergency responders (EMS, police, fire). Dispatchers use standardized scripts to ensure effective communication and rapid response coordination.

#### Human Resources  
Supports recruitment, development, regulation, and retention of EMS personnel. HR ensures fair compensation and seamless integration into broader community systems.

#### Medical Director  
Every EMS system is led by a physician medical director who authorizes clinical decisions. The medical director approves both **offline** (pre-established) and **online** (real-time via medical control) treatment protocols. They serve as the liaison between hospitals, physicians, and EMS providers.

**The Circulatory System: Physiology**

Blood pressure (BP) is the force of circulating blood against the walls of the body. When the left ventricle of the heart contracts, it pumps blood into the aorta. This phase in the cardiac cycle is called **systole**. The pressure inside the arteries during this time is referred to as the systolic blood pressure. The time between contractions when the ventricle is relaxed and refilling with blood is called **diastole**. The resting pressure in the arteries during this phase is the diastolic blood pressure. The values of the systolic and diastolic pressures are measured with a sphygmomanometer (blood pressure cuff) and are expressed numerically in millimeters of mercury (mm Hg). Blood pressure is displayed as the systolic number over the diastolic number.
