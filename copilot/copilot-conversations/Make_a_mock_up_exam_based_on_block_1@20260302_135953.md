1. Cumulative stress occurs when EMS providers are exposed to prolonged or excessive stress, with each event making recovery more difficult. Symptoms include fatigue, changes in appetite, headaches, insomnia, and irritability. [^3]  
2. Scene Size-Up steps include BSI/PPE, identifying environmental hazards (e.g., fire, weapons), ensuring personal safety, assessing the number of patients, evaluating mechanism of injury, determining additional resources needed, and checking for specialized equipment requirements. [^2]  
3. Anatomy studies the physical structure of body systems; physiology examines normal functions such as respiration or circulation. In EMS, understanding anatomy helps identify injuries, while physiology guides how the body responds to trauma or illness. [^16]  
4. Expressed consent is a patient’s explicit agreement (e.g., nodding yes), while informed consent involves clear communication of risks, benefits, and alternatives before treatment begins. Example: A patient nods in agreement to care → expressed consent; a provider explains the risks of CPR and asks if they want it → informed consent. [^17]  
5. Handover is error-prone due to information loss or distortion during transfers between providers. Steps to minimize errors include managing environment, reducing noise, ensuring ABCs are maintained, providing structured reports (e.g., SBAR), and confirming all details with the receiving provider. **ai**: Toxoplasmosis is a parasitic infection caused by *Toxoplasma gondii*, a protozoan that can infect both humans and animals [^1]. It is primarily transmitted through ingestion of oocysts from contaminated soil, undercooked meat, or cat feces, as well as vertically during pregnancy [^2].

The disease often presents asymptomatically in healthy individuals but may lead to severe complications such as encephalitis, retinal inflammation, or congenital abnormalities when acquired during early gestation [^3]. In immunocompromised patients, toxoplasmosis can manifest with disseminated infection and multi-organ involvement.

Diagnosis relies on serological testing (e.g., IgG and IgM antibodies) and imaging studies such as MRI or CT scans in symptomatic cases. Treatment typically involves antiparasitic medications like pyrimethamine combined with sulfadiazine and folinic acid, especially in severe or immunocompromised individuals [^4].

Prevention strategies include avoiding raw meat consumption, washing fruits and vegetables thoroughly, practicing good hygiene when handling soil or cat litter, and ensuring proper cooking of food [^5].
