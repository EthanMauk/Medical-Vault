## Overview
Emergency Medical Services **(EMS)** is a system of prehospital medical health providers and professionals that provide emergency care and transport to the sick and injured. EMS clinicians also provide [[preventative education]] and [[preventative care]], early crisis identification, and healthcare to the community.  

## History 
EMS as a modern organization in the United States originates from the publication of 1966's *[[Accidental Death and Disability]]*: which highlighted to the public and congress the inadequacies of prehospital care, and the lengths [[BLS]] and [[ALS]] in the initial few minutes of an emergency can go towards saving the patients life. This led to the [[Highway Safety Act of 1966]] which established federal leadership in EMS to reduce traffic related deaths through its creation of the [[National Highway Traffic Safety Administration ]](NHTSA) which set standards for ambulance equipment, communication and personnel. In the 1970's the Department of Transportation developed the first curriculum for EMT training. Since the 1990's there has been a federal push towards regulation and standardization of the levels of EMS education to help providers work around the country. In 2019 the [[National Highway Traffic Safety Administration|NHTSA]]'s EMS Agenda 2050 reaffirmed this goal as well the intention to make EMS a field people want to work in.

### [[Paramedic]]
Paramedics are trained extensively in theoretical knowledge and skills to provide [[ALS]] care. Training is typically 2000-2500 hours.
### Dispatch 
Public access to EMS often starts at a 9-1-1 dispatcher. Communication centers across the states connect the public to EMS, police, fire, and other resources to respond to an emergency. Dispatchers are trained and provided scripts to help them integrate into EMS. 
### Human Resources
Human resources support the recruitment, development, regulation, and retention of EMS providers. They help determine how EMS workers are compensated and how they integrate into other systems. 
### Medical Director
Every EMS system has a physician medical director who authorizes EMT's to provide medical care. They are responsible for delegating [[offline]] and [[online]] authorization for treatment. The medical director is a liaison between the medical community, hospitals and EMS workers. 

## National EMS Scope of Practice Model 
National guidelines from the NHTSA outline the minimum skills each level of EMS provider should be able to accomplish and the progression from one provider to the next level. Since licensure is done by the state, the state decides how EMS providers will operate. Local medical directors will provide oversight to EMS personnel and have standing orders on day to day operational concerns. Expanding the scope of practice requires state and medical directors approval.
### Basic Life Support Certified 
Millions of people laypeople are trained in Basic Life Support. These people will often be first on the scene and be able to provide [[Cardiopulmonary Resusitation|CPR]], use [[Automated External Defibrillators|AEDs]], and perform basic bleeding control procedures such as a tourniquet. Training is typically is 4-10 hours and includes Teachers, Coaches, Security Personnel. 
### [[EMR|Emergency Medical Responder]]
EMR's are first responders who are trained in immediate care skills and integration with EMT's. Training is typically 50 hours. 
### [[EMT|Emergency Medical Technician ]]
EMT's are responsible for assessing, treating, packaging and transporting patients to the hospital. They are responsible for establishing and following [[chain of command]] and [[Continuity of Care]] for their patients. Training is typically 200-250 hours.  

## Professional Attributes of an EMS Worker
| Attribute                   | Why It Matters                                                                               |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Integrity**               | Upholds trust in high-stress environments; acts with honor even under pressure               |
| **Empathy**                 | Recognizes the trauma of emergency situations — helps de-escalate distress and build rapport |
| **Self-Motivated Learning** | Continuously updates knowledge to stay ahead of evolving medical practices                   |
| **Hygiene & Appearance**    | Projects professionalism, which builds patient confidence and trust                          |
| **Time Management**         | Balances rapid decision-making with efficient task execution in chaotic environments         |
| **Teamwork**                | Communicates clearly across teams; delegates effectively during emergencies                  |
| **Patient Advocacy**        | Prioritizes the patient’s needs                                                              |
| **Self-Confidence**         | Knows what they _can_ and _cannot_ do — avoids overreach while maintaining competence        |
The public has very little contact with EMS, and when it is it is often on the worst day of their lives. Any measures that can be taken to ease the experience for them, from speaking in a calm and controlled tone to allowing them time to speak and think through their situation can help prevent further and unnecessary [[Distress]] and foster positive community relations and education.
